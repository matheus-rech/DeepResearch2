TI  - Deep learning approaches for systematic literature review automation: A comprehensive analysis.
PG  - 123-135
LID - 10.1234/example.2023.12345 [doi]
AB  - BACKGROUND: Systematic literature reviews are essential for evidence-based medicine but are time-consuming and resource-intensive. OBJECTIVE: To evaluate the effectiveness of deep learning approaches in automating systematic literature review processes. METHODS: We conducted a comprehensive analysis of 150 studies published between 2018-2023 that utilized machine learning for literature screening. RESULTS: Deep learning models achieved 92% accuracy in title/abstract screening compared to 78% for traditional keyword-based approaches. CONCLUSION: AI-powered systematic review tools show significant promise for reducing manual workload while maintaining high accuracy.

TI  - Machine learning for clinical decision support in systematic reviews: A randomized controlled trial.
PG  - 89-102
LID - 10.5678/clinical.2023.23456 [doi]
AB  - INTRODUCTION: Clinical decision support systems require high-quality evidence synthesis. OBJECTIVE: To assess whether machine learning-assisted systematic reviews improve clinical decision-making accuracy. DESIGN: Randomized controlled trial with 200 clinicians. INTERVENTION: Half received ML-assisted systematic review summaries, half received traditional reviews. MAIN OUTCOME: Diagnostic accuracy in clinical scenarios. RESULTS: ML-assisted group showed 15% improvement in diagnostic accuracy (p<0.001). CONCLUSION: Machine learning significantly enhances clinical decision support through improved evidence synthesis.

TI  - Natural language processing for automated screening in systematic reviews: Performance evaluation across medical domains.
PG  - 45-58
LID - 10.9012/nlp.2023.34567 [doi]
AB  - PURPOSE: To evaluate natural language processing (NLP) performance for automated screening across different medical specialties. METHODS: We tested 5 NLP models on 10,000 abstracts from cardiology, oncology, neurology, and psychiatry systematic reviews. METRICS: Sensitivity, specificity, and F1-scores were calculated for each domain. RESULTS: BERT-based models achieved highest performance (F1=0.89) in cardiology, while domain-specific models performed better in psychiatry (F1=0.91). LIMITATIONS: Performance varied significantly across medical domains. CONCLUSIONS: NLP shows promise for systematic review automation but requires domain-specific optimization.
